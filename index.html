About me

Nelly Vanessa Padilla Bello

Postdoctoral Researcher · Department of Dentistry · University Paris Cité – CentraleSupélec

Hello! My name is Nelly Vanessa Padilla Bello and I am a postdoctoral researcher at the University of Paris Cité, in collaboration with the LMPS laboratory at CentraleSupélec, Paris, France.

I hold a Ph.D. in materials science with experience in multimodal and multiscale characterization of materials.

My current project focuses on the characterization of pathological tissues at different scales to understand the impact of disease on the physicochemistry and microstructure of tissues. The goal is to adapt laboratory techniques to clinical applications and ultimately support dentists in improving diagnosis and treatment decisions.

I also teach practical courses (TP) in microstructure characterization, with a focus on bone microstructure and mechanics, and supervise student projects across various subjects.

Influence of basis weight and microfibril dimensions of microfibrillated cellulose (MFC) films on the microstructure, adhesion, and barrier properties of wet-laminated paper materials.

PADILLA BELLO N. V. · Rota M. · Curmi H. · Rol F. · Rolland du Roscoat S. · Geindreau C. Packaging Technology and Science Journal, 2026

Investigated how MFC film basis weight and microfibril size affect the microstructure, adhesion, and barrier performance of cellulose-based multilayers using multimodal 3D characterization. 03

Multiscale characterisation of cellulose nanofibril networks using three 3D imaging methods

PADILLA BELLO N. V. · Rota M. · Curmi H. · Rol F · Douillard T. · Geindreau C. · Rolland du Roscoat S. Journal of Microscopy, 2025

This work investigates the 3D microstructure of cellulose-based bilayer materials for sustainable food packaging using a multiscale imaging approach combining synchrotron X-ray micro-/nanotomography and FIB-SEM tomography. The study reveals that MFC films have a dense, non-connected pore structure and shows that films made with finer microfibrils exhibit lower porosity, a more homogeneous microstructure, and greater contact area with the paper, contributing to improved barrier properties.

04

Uncertainty in the determination of the volumetric block proportion of BIMrocks/ BIMsoils from one-dimensional information

Ramos-Cañón A. F. · Castro-Malaver L. C · PADILLA-BELLO N.V · Vega-Posada C. A. Boletin de Geologia., 2020

This work investigates the uncertainty in estimating the Proportion of Volumetric Blocks (PVB) in heterogeneous Block-in-Matrix (BIMrock/BIMsoil) materials from borehole data. A computational algorithm was developed to assess how borehole number and length, as well as block shape and orientation, affect PVB estimation. The results show that increasing the extent of subsurface investigations reduces epistemic uncertainty regardless of block geometry, but a minimum level of uncertainty remains due to the inherent randomness of the material's natural formation.

🔬 What is Porosity—and Why Does It Matter in Paper? 🧵

When we zoom into a sheet of paper, we see more than just fibers. We see spaces — tiny voids between fibers — and these voids are called pores. 📌 Porosity refers to the volume of these empty spaces compared to the total volume of the material. Far from being just a random feature, porosity is a key player in how material performs, in this case paper: 💧 It determines how much water or ink the paper can absorb 💨 It influences how gases and vapors travel through it 🧪 It even affects coating performance and printability Porosity is typically measured using: Mercury intrusion porosimetry (for accessible pores) Gas permeability tests Imaging methods (like 3D X-ray tomography 🧠 — my personal favorite!) High porosity = more air = better absorbency but worse barrier properties. Low porosity = tighter structure = better barriers, but maybe less flexibility. Understanding porosity helps us design better materials—especially in sustainable packaging, where barrier performance is key. 🌍📦 👉 In my next post, I'll explore how microstructure can be tailored to tune porosity and barrier properties. Have you worked with porous materials before? How did you measure or control porosity?

When Even X-ray Nano-Tomography Isn't Enough… Meet FIB-SEM Tomography

In my last post, I talked about 3D X-ray tomography and how it helps us visualize the internal structure of materials —like paper from pores to fiber networks to coating layers. Synchrotron X-ray nano-tomography gives stunning resolution (~25 nm/pixel), but sometimes… we need to go even further. So what if we want to see beyond that, at just a few nanometers — while still building a 3D reconstruction? 🔬 That's where FIB-SEM tomography comes in. FIB-SEM stands for Focused Ion Beam – Scanning Electron Microscopy. It works by: 1️⃣ Slicing the sample nanometer by nanometer with an ion beam 2️⃣ Imaging each slice with an electron beam 3️⃣ Reconstructing the structure in 3D ✅ Resolution: down to ~5 nm ✅ Ideal for observing dense, nano-structured materials like MFC-based films ✅ Gives insights into pore geometry, interfaces, coating homogeneity, and nanoscale defects ⚠️ Yes — it's destructive, and the volumes are small. But the level of detail is exceptional. 💡 This technique helps us understand how nanofibers pack, how coatings spread, and what might be affecting barrier performance — from the inside. ➡️ In my next post, I'll show you an example of a 3D reconstruction of paper and how we extract pore information from it. Have you ever worked with FIB-SEM or nanoscale imaging? What was your biggest challenge?
