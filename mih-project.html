Towards a quantitative diagnosis of MIH

Combining Raman spectroscopy and Optical Coherence Tomography to support MIH diagnosis

Background

Molar-Incisor Hypomineralization (MIH) is a dental condition affecting enamel, characterized by well-defined opacities and alterations in microstructure, chemical composition, mineral density, and mechanical properties [1]. Diagnosis of MIH is typically based on visual examination and its treatment depends on lesion severity and depth, which cannot be assessed using current clinical tools. To address these limitations, we combine two non-invasive techniques: Raman spectroscopy to assess the physico-chemical alterations [2] and optical coherence tomography (OCT) to investigate lesion location [3]. The aim is to identify reliable criteria of distinction from Raman spectra and assess the OCT’s ability to delineate MIH-lesions. .

Methods

This project combines complementary imaging and spectroscopic techniques to investigate the structure and composition of healthy and MIH-affected teeth. Techniques include: 1. To support the diagnosis: Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) for non-destructive imaging of enamel microstructure. It reveals the internal structure of MIH. Raman spectroscopy to characterize the chemical composition and mineralization of dental tissues. 2. To characterize the material and better understand the pathology Micro-computed tomography (micro-CT) to quantify mineral density in three dimensions. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) to observe enamel microstructure at high resolution. Statistical analysis to correlate measurements obtained from the different techniques and identify quantitative markers of MIH severity.

Results

Raman spectroscopy revealed more pronounced amide and CH peaks for MIH than for healthy enamel. Amide/phosphate and CH/phosphate ratios were both able to discriminate MIH from healthy enamel. CH maps also revealed low variability within MIH lesions despite the tissue heterogeneity. OCT revealed contrast in tissue architecture, with affected enamel showing stronger surface reflection and higher OAC. Raman spectroscopy suggested an increased protein content and a reduction in mineral density, consistent with the structural difference observed in the MIH lesions. OCT observations agreed with the reported heterogeneity of MIH lesions causing light scattering, and opened perspectives to delineate MIH lesions. Overall, despite the low number of samples, these findings highlight the potential of integrating OCT and Raman spectroscopy for an objective quantitative, and multi-scale characterization of MIH to enhance diagnosis accuracy in both research and clinical settings.

Perspectives

Future work will extend these multimodal characterization methods to an animal model of MIH, providing a controlled framework to validate quantitative biomarkers of enamel defects. This approach will enable a more systematic investigation of the relationships between mineralization, microstructure, and chemical composition during lesion development. In the long term, the knowledge gained from these studies may contribute to the development of objective, non-invasive diagnostic tools for the clinical assessment of MIH.
